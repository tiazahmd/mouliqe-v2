Executive summary

In physical retail, the point of sale is the most valuable real estate in the store. It is where purchase decisions are made, where brand visibility converts to revenue, and where placement strategy either works or fails. Yet in most emerging markets, there is no systematic data on what actually happens there. Placement decisions are made on intuition, not evidence.

This document proposes a three-phase initiative that transforms this blind spot into a proprietary competitive advantage. The vision begins with data collection and ends with a scalable product:

Phase 1 — The Pilot

Install cameras at 50–100 retail locations. Capture rich shopper behavior data — foot traffic, dwell time, gaze direction, attention heatmaps, product interaction — using an AI-powered computer vision pipeline. Build the labeled dataset that maps store layouts to attention patterns. All processing happens on-site; raw footage never leaves the store.

Phase 2 — The Model

Use the pilot data to develop a proprietary algorithm that predicts optimal product placement based on store geometry, layout type, and customer interaction patterns. The model learns what the cameras observed — which positions in a given layout maximize visual attention and engagement.

Phase 3 — The Product

A standalone tool for field teams. Upload a photo of any retail location or POS area, answer a few questions about dimensions and layout, and the system outputs ideal placement recommendations. No cameras. No on-site hardware. No per-store installation. Placement optimization scaled across the entire retail network.

The platform is delivered through a strategic partnership between Mouliqe (AI architecture, computer vision, model development, and product engineering) and Parlance Consulting Services Ltd. (market research methodology, field operations, retailer relationships, and regulatory compliance). The client receives a unified deliverable across all three phases.

Guiding principles

Privacy by architecture

Raw video never enters the data pipeline. Anonymization at the edge. The central platform only receives structured, non-identifiable data.

Pilot as investment

Phase 1 is not just data collection — it is training data acquisition. Every design decision is made with Phase 2 model development in mind.

Research-grade, honestly framed

Camera-based gaze estimation is directional intelligence, not lab precision. Accuracy reported transparently and calibrated against ground-truth.

Built to scale

Phase 3 eliminates cameras entirely. The tool works from a photo and a questionnaire. Every retail location in the network gets data-driven placement guidance.

The opportunity

The global in-store analytics market was valued at $5.3 billion in 2023 and is projected to reach $16.5 billion by 2030, growing at a compound annual rate of 21.8%. This growth is driven by a simple reality: physical retail has operated without the behavioral intelligence that digital commerce has had for over a decade.

In digital retail, every interaction is instrumented. Click-through rates, scroll depth, hover duration, session recordings, and conversion funnels provide a complete picture of how shoppers engage with products and content. In physical retail — particularly in emerging markets — the equivalent data does not exist. There are no heatmaps showing where shoppers look. No dwell-time analysis showing which displays hold attention. No A/B testing framework for comparing placement strategies.

The consequence is that every decision about product placement, signage positioning, and display design at the point of sale is a hypothesis that cannot be validated. Brands invest in POS materials based on convention, intuition, and periodic manual audits.

Advances in computer vision, pose estimation, and gaze inference have changed what is possible. It is now feasible to extract rich behavioral data from standard camera footage — and then go further: use that data to train a predictive model that eliminates the need for cameras entirely. The technology exists. What has been missing is a platform designed for the constraints of emerging-market retail and a vision that extends beyond data collection to a scalable product.

What we capture in Phase 1

The platform extracts a full spectrum of shopper behavior metrics from standard camera footage. Each metric feeds both the Phase 1 analytics dashboards and the Phase 2 model training pipeline.

Foot Traffic & Flow

Visitor count, entry/exit patterns, peak hours, movement paths through the retail space.

Dwell Time Analysis

Time spent in specific zones — counter, product displays, signage. Strong predictor of purchase intent.

Gaze Direction

Head pose and gaze estimation — which shelf sections, product tiers, and signage capture visual attention.

Attention Heatmaps

Aggregated visual attention rendered as spatial heatmaps. Hot zones and dead zones across the store.

Product Interaction

Pick-up, examination, and put-back detection. Gap between visual attention and physical engagement.

Demographic Segments

Anonymous age-range and gender estimation. No facial recognition — aggregate statistical classification only.

Queue & Purchase

Queue time, purchase patterns, POS interaction duration. Correlates wait time with browsing behavior.

Temporal Patterns

Behavioral variation by time of day, day of week, and season. Peak attention windows.

Layout–Attention Mapping

The key Phase 2 input: correlating store geometry and product positions with measured attention data.

The vision — three phases

The initiative is structured as three sequential phases, each building on the outputs of the previous one. Phase 1 produces the data. Phase 2 produces the intelligence. Phase 3 produces the product.

Phase 1 — The Pilot
Data Collection & Behavioral Analytics

Install cameras at 50–100 retail locations across diverse store formats. Run the computer vision pipeline to capture foot traffic, dwell time, gaze direction, attention heatmaps, product interaction, and demographic segmentation. All processing happens on-site using portable hardware — raw footage never leaves the store. The output is twofold: (1) analytics dashboards and research reports for immediate business value, and (2) a labeled dataset mapping store layouts to measured attention patterns — the training data for Phase 2.

Output: behavioral analytics + labeled training dataset

Phase 2 — The Model
Proprietary Placement Optimization Algorithm

Train a proprietary algorithm on the Phase 1 dataset. The model learns the relationship between store geometry (dimensions, layout type, counter position, shelf configuration) and human attention (where shoppers look, how long they engage, what they interact with, what they ignore). The output is a predictive model that takes store layout parameters as input and outputs optimal placement recommendations — without needing cameras, on-site hardware, or behavioral observation. The model encodes what the cameras learned.

Output: placement optimization algorithm (the core IP)

Phase 3 — The Product
Scalable Placement Recommendation Tool

Package the model into a standalone tool for field teams. A field representative uploads a photo of a retail location or POS area, answers a few questions (square footage, layout type, product categories, traffic estimate), and the system outputs a visual placement recommendation map — where to position products, signage, and displays for maximum shopper attention. Recommendations are ranked by predicted impact and annotated with confidence scores. No cameras. No installation. No per-store hardware. Placement optimization scaled to every retail location in the network.

Output: scalable placement tool for the entire retail network

Continuous improvement

The model improves with scale. Every new store format and every new retail location adds training data. As the network expands, the algorithm becomes more accurate and more generalizable — delivering better placement recommendations with each new location onboarded.

Phase 1 — the pilot

The pilot uses a batch processing model designed for reliability, privacy, and infrastructure constraints. No live streaming, no cloud dependency, no internet required at the retail site.

01
Capture
Strategically positioned cameras record shopper activity during operating hours. Footage stored locally on-site. Camera placement optimized per store layout to maximize coverage of POS zones, product displays, and high-traffic areas.
02
Process
A portable processing unit runs the full computer vision pipeline on-site: person detection, multi-object tracking, 3D skeleton reconstruction, head pose estimation, gaze inference, zone-based dwell analysis, and product interaction detection. Raw footage never leaves the store.
03
Extract
The pipeline outputs structured, anonymized data points — not video. Timestamps, zone attention scores, dwell durations, gaze heatmap coordinates, interaction events, and demographic estimates. All PII discarded during processing.
04
Analyze & Label
Data from all sites aggregated into the central analytics platform. Dashboards, heatmaps, trend reports, and A/B comparisons for immediate business value. Simultaneously, the data is structured and labeled for Phase 2 model training — correlating store geometry with measured attention patterns.

Phase 2 — the model

The pilot data becomes the training set for a proprietary placement optimization algorithm. Phase 2 transforms observational data into predictive intelligence — a model that can recommend optimal product placement without needing cameras or on-site observation.

What goes in

The model is trained on the labeled dataset produced during Phase 1, combining two categories of input:

Store geometry

Dimensions, layout type, counter position, shelf configuration, entry/exit points, display locations, traffic flow patterns. Captured from store layout documentation and camera-derived spatial analysis.

Measured attention data

Gaze direction vectors, dwell time per zone, attention heatmaps, product interaction events, demographic-segmented behavior patterns. Captured from the Phase 1 computer vision pipeline across 50–100 stores.

What the model learns

The algorithm learns the relationship between physical space and human attention. Specifically:

Position → attention mapping

Which positions in a given layout type maximize visual engagement. How shelf height, counter proximity, and display angle affect gaze probability.

Layout → flow prediction

How store geometry affects traffic flow and natural gaze patterns. Where shoppers look first, where attention drops off, and which spatial configurations create attention dead zones.

Format generalization

Patterns that hold across store formats — from small kiosks to large retail outlets. Which placement principles are universal and which are format-specific.

Demographic variation

How attention patterns differ by demographic segment. Whether placement optimization should vary by the primary customer profile of a given store.

What comes out

A predictive algorithm that takes store layout parameters as input and outputs optimal placement recommendations — without needing cameras, on-site hardware, or behavioral observation. The model encodes what the cameras learned. It is the core intellectual property of the initiative and the foundation for the Phase 3 product.

Why it improves over time

Every new store and every new layout format adds training data. The model becomes more accurate and more generalizable as the retail network expands. This is not a static algorithm — it is a learning system that continuously improves as more locations are onboarded.

Phase 3 — the product

Phase 3 packages the proprietary model into a standalone tool that eliminates the need for cameras, on-site hardware, and per-store installation. The tool makes placement optimization accessible to every retail location in the network — at the cost of uploading a photo and answering a few questions.

How it works
Step 1 — Upload a photo

A field representative takes a photo of the retail location or POS area. The system uses computer vision to analyze the spatial layout — identifying shelves, counter positions, display areas, and traffic entry points. No special equipment or camera angle required.

Step 2 — Answer a few questions

Store square footage, layout type, primary product categories, estimated daily traffic volume, and any placement constraints. A simple form designed for non-technical users — field representatives, merchandisers, or store managers.

Step 3 — Get placement recommendations

The model outputs a visual placement map: where to position products, signage, and displays for maximum shopper attention. Recommendations are ranked by predicted impact and annotated with confidence scores. The output is immediately actionable — a field representative can implement the recommendations on the spot.

Scaling across the retail network

The pilot covers 50–100 stores with cameras. The product covers every store without them. New locations are onboarded in minutes — a photo and a questionnaire — giving field teams data-driven placement guidance at every retail touchpoint in the network. The model learns patterns across diverse store formats, making it effective whether the location is a small roadside kiosk or a large retail outlet.

Small kiosks & roadside shops

Compact POS environments where counter space is limited and every placement decision has outsized impact on visibility.

Convenience stores

Mid-size retail with multiple product zones, counter displays, and shelf sections competing for shopper attention.

Large retail outlets

Complex layouts with multiple aisles, end-caps, and dedicated display areas requiring zone-specific optimization.

New markets & regions

As the network expands geographically, the model adapts to new store formats and regional layout patterns without starting from scratch.

The end state

Every field representative in the network can optimize POS placement in minutes — without hiring consultants, installing cameras, or running observational studies. The tool does what used to require weeks of fieldwork. That is the value of Phase 3.
