Executive summary

In physical retail, the point of sale is the most valuable real estate in the store. It is where purchase decisions are made, where brand visibility converts to revenue, and where placement strategy either works or fails. Yet in most emerging markets, there is no systematic data on what actually happens there.

E-commerce platforms track every click, scroll, and hover. They generate heatmaps, session recordings, and conversion funnels that tell brands exactly where attention goes and where it doesn't. Physical retail has no equivalent. Placement decisions — where to position products, signage, and displays — are made on intuition, historical convention, and anecdotal feedback. The cost of this blind spot is unmeasured but significant: brands invest heavily in point-of-sale materials with no way to validate whether shoppers actually see them, engage with them, or ignore them entirely.

Modern computer vision and AI make it possible to close this gap. Using strategically placed cameras and advanced behavioral analytics, it is now feasible to capture the same depth of shopper behavior data in a physical retail environment that digital platforms have had for decades — without requiring shoppers to wear devices, answer surveys, or change their behavior in any way.

This document proposes a POS Shopper Behavior Analytics Platform: an AI-powered computer vision system designed to capture, analyze, and report in-store shopper behavior across a pilot network of retail locations. The platform is designed for batch processing (no live streaming or cloud dependency), on-site privacy (raw footage never leaves the store), and scalability (from a 50-store pilot to nationwide deployment).

The platform is delivered through a strategic partnership between Mouliqe (AI architecture, computer vision platform, and analytics engine) and Parlance Consulting Services Ltd. (market research methodology, field operations, retailer relationships, and regulatory compliance). The client receives a unified deliverable — not two separate workstreams.

Guiding principles

Privacy by architecture

Raw video never enters the data pipeline. Anonymization happens at the edge, during processing. The central platform only receives structured, non-identifiable data points.

Batch, not live

No internet required at retail sites. Footage is recorded locally and processed on a scheduled basis using portable hardware. Designed for infrastructure-constrained environments.

Research-grade, honestly framed

Camera-based gaze estimation is directional intelligence, not laboratory precision. Outputs are framed as behavioral signals, calibrated against ground-truth, with accuracy reported transparently.

Built to scale

Per-store configuration without code changes. A small kiosk and a large retail outlet use the same platform with different spatial parameters. Designed for 50 stores, architected for thousands.

The opportunity

The global in-store analytics market was valued at $5.3 billion in 2023 and is projected to reach $16.5 billion by 2030, growing at a compound annual rate of 21.8%. This growth is driven by a simple reality: physical retail has operated without the behavioral intelligence that digital commerce has had for over a decade.

In digital retail, every interaction is instrumented. Click-through rates, scroll depth, hover duration, session recordings, and conversion funnels provide a complete picture of how shoppers engage with products and content. In physical retail — particularly in emerging markets — the equivalent data does not exist. There are no heatmaps showing where shoppers look. No dwell-time analysis showing which displays hold attention. No A/B testing framework for comparing product placement strategies.

The consequence is that every decision about product placement, signage positioning, and display design at the point of sale is a hypothesis that cannot be validated. Brands invest in POS materials based on convention, intuition, and periodic manual audits. The gap between what is spent on POS strategy and what is known about its effectiveness is significant — and until recently, it was technically impractical to close.

Advances in computer vision, pose estimation, and gaze inference have changed this. It is now possible to extract rich behavioral data from standard camera footage without requiring shoppers to wear devices, participate in studies, or alter their natural behavior. The technology exists. What has been missing is a platform designed specifically for the constraints of emerging-market retail: variable infrastructure, batch processing requirements, strict privacy needs, and the ability to scale across hundreds of diverse store formats.

03 —

What we capture

The platform extracts a full spectrum of shopper behavior metrics from standard camera footage. Each metric is produced through the computer vision pipeline described in Section 04, with no wearable devices or shopper participation required.

Foot Traffic & Flow

Visitor count, entry/exit patterns, peak hours, movement paths through the retail space.

Dwell Time Analysis

Time spent in specific zones — counter, product displays, signage areas. Strong predictor of purchase intent.

Gaze Direction

Head pose and gaze estimation — which shelf sections, product tiers, and signage capture visual attention.

Attention Heatmaps

Aggregated visual attention rendered as spatial heatmaps. Identifies hot zones and dead zones across the store.

Product Interaction

Pick-up, examination, and put-back detection. Measures the gap between visual attention and physical engagement.

Demographic Segments

Anonymous age-range and gender estimation for behavioral segmentation. No facial recognition or PII.

Queue & Purchase

Queue time, purchase completion patterns, POS interaction duration. Correlates wait time with browsing behavior.

Temporal Patterns

Behavioral variation by time of day, day of week, and season. Identifies peak attention windows.

A/B Placement Testing

Compare metrics across store groups with different configurations. Statistically validate which arrangements work.

How it works

The platform uses a batch processing model designed for reliability, privacy, and infrastructure constraints. There is no live streaming, no cloud dependency, and no internet required at the retail site. Raw footage never leaves the store.

01
Capture
Strategically positioned cameras record shopper activity during operating hours. Footage is stored locally on-site using standard storage devices. Camera placement is optimized per store layout to maximize coverage of POS zones, product displays, and high-traffic areas. No internet connectivity is required at the retail site.
02
Process
A portable processing unit is brought to the retail site on a scheduled basis. The unit runs the full computer vision pipeline locally: person detection and multi-object tracking, 3D skeleton reconstruction for body pose and head orientation, gaze inference from head pose estimation, zone-based dwell analysis with configurable regions of interest, and product interaction detection. All processing happens on-site. Raw footage is never transferred, uploaded, or transmitted.
03
Extract
The processing pipeline outputs structured, anonymized data points — not video. Timestamps, zone attention scores, dwell durations, gaze heatmap coordinates, interaction events, and demographic segment estimates are extracted into a standardized schema. All personally identifiable information is discarded during processing. The output is a structured dataset that contains no images, no video frames, and no biometric data.
04
Analyze & Report
Extracted data from all retail sites is aggregated into the central analytics platform. Interactive dashboards provide filtering by store, zone, time period, and demographic segment. Spatial attention heatmaps are overlaid on store layout diagrams. A/B comparison tools enable testing placement changes across store groups. Automated report generation produces periodic research deliverables. Raw data exports and API access are available for integration with existing business intelligence platforms.
Privacy architecture

Privacy is not a policy layer added after the system is built — it is an architectural decision. The data pipeline is designed so that raw video never enters it. Anonymization happens at the edge, during processing. The central analytics platform only receives structured, non-identifiable data points. This is not a feature of the system. It is how the system is designed.

The platform

Three layers — edge processing, data pipeline, and analytics — designed to scale from a 50-store pilot to nationwide deployment. Each layer is independently configurable per store without code changes.

L1
Edge Processing Engine

The core computer vision pipeline running on portable hardware. Processes recorded footage on-site and outputs structured behavioral data.

Person detection (state-of-the-art real-time models)
Multi-object tracking across frames
3D skeleton reconstruction
Head pose estimation
Gaze inference from head orientation
Zone-based spatial analysis
Product interaction detection
Demographic classification (anonymous)
L2
Data Pipeline & Storage

Structured data extraction with full anonymization at the edge. Standardized schema across all retail sites for consistent aggregation and historical analysis.

Edge anonymization — no raw video in pipeline
Standardized output schema across all sites
Batch upload from portable units
Central data warehouse
Historical trend storage
Data validation and quality checks
L3
Analytics & Reporting

Interactive dashboards, spatial heatmaps, and automated reporting. Designed for both research analysts and business stakeholders.

Interactive dashboards with multi-dimensional filtering
Spatial attention heatmaps on store layouts
A/B comparison tools for placement testing
Automated periodic report generation
Raw data export (CSV, JSON)
API access for BI platform integration
Per-store configuration

Every retail environment is different. The platform supports per-store configuration: camera positions, zone definitions, region-of-interest boundaries, and analysis parameters are all adjustable without code changes. A small roadside kiosk and a large retail outlet use the same platform with different spatial configurations. New stores are onboarded by defining their layout and zone map — not by writing custom code.

Next steps

This document outlines the platform architecture and partnership model at a conceptual level. The following steps move the engagement from concept to execution.

1. Define pilot parameters

Number of retail sites, store types, geographic distribution, pilot duration, and specific research questions to be answered. These parameters drive hardware specifications, processing schedules, and analytics configuration.

2. Technical blueprint

A detailed technical architecture document covering hardware specifications, software stack, data schema, processing pipeline design, dashboard wireframes, and deployment plan. This is the engineering specification that turns the conceptual platform into a buildable system.

3. Pilot site assessment

On-site evaluation of representative retail locations to determine camera placement, power and storage requirements, physical constraints, and site-specific configuration needs. Conducted jointly by Parlance (field logistics) and Mouliqe (technical requirements).

4. Scope, timeline, and investment

With pilot parameters and site assessment complete, Mouliqe and Parlance will deliver a detailed scope of work with timeline, milestones, and investment breakdown. No estimates without evidence — the numbers will be grounded in the actual requirements discovered during the previous steps.
