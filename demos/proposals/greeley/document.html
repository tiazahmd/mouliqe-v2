Executive summary

The City of Greeley is at an inflection point. After a year of building an Innovation and High Performance department, investing in enterprise AI tools, and modernizing citizen-facing platforms, the city has made meaningful progress toward a technology-forward future. At the same time, budget reductions and staffing constraints are creating pressure to demonstrate value quickly and do more with less.

This is not a technology problem. Greeley already has capable tools in place — an enterprise-wide GovAI license with 200 active users, Microsoft Copilot on the public website, a Zoom-powered 311 call center with virtual agent capabilities, Oracle ERP in the cloud, Cityworks for asset and work order management, ArcGIS for geospatial data, Granicus OneView for citizen request management, and a growing Azure footprint. The challenge is that these investments are operating in isolation. There is no unified data layer connecting them, no dedicated ownership of integration and optimization, and no governance framework ensuring the accuracy and reliability of AI-generated outputs.

The gap is compounded by a workforce reality that many cities share but few address directly: Greeley's departments span desk-based IT and administrative staff to field crews in public works and public safety who have no on-site access to a computer. Any AI strategy that only serves the desk-bound half of the organization will fail to deliver the broad-based enablement that leadership is looking for.

What Greeley needs is not another tool. It needs a structured approach to connecting what already exists, validating what AI produces, and building the internal capacity to maintain and evolve these systems over time. The goal is not to replace anyone — it is to make lean teams more capable, help leadership tell the investment story with real data, and create a foundation that scales.

Phase 1 delivers four things: a comprehensive current-state assessment, a working AI-powered work order prioritization tool, a suite of monitoring and reporting dashboards that deliver immediate operational value across departments, and a governance framework with an executive business case that gives leadership the data to justify continued investment. It is scoped to be achievable within existing budget constraints, delivered as a flat-fee engagement with no ongoing dependency. Phases 2 and 3 provide a roadmap toward unified data infrastructure in Azure that connects all major city systems.

Guiding principles

Build within, not on top of

Work entirely within Greeley's existing Azure, Oracle, and GovAI investments — no new platforms.

Enablement, not replacement

AI as a tool that makes existing teams more capable — every recommendation evaluated against this standard.

Working results in Phase 1

Deployed tools and dashboards, not just a report — making the business case for continued investment with real evidence.

Capacity, not dependency

Interactive documentation so internal teams maintain and evolve what was built without re-engaging the consultant.

Current state assessment

Organizational context

The City of Greeley's Innovation and High Performance department is approximately one year old, operating under the CIO's office with a mandate to improve efficiency, technology adoption, and cross-department collaboration. The department is led by Kylie Jackson, who reports to CIO Bret Naber and oversees analysts, project managers, and the city's project management office.

The department is building in a constrained environment. Budget reductions and staff cuts are active across the organization. There is no dedicated data engineering capacity within the city, and the IT team lacks a designated owner for AI integration and API connectivity work. These constraints are not unique to Greeley — but they define the practical boundaries of what can be accomplished without structured external support.

At the same time, the city has made meaningful progress. An AI policy is in place. An enterprise-wide generative AI platform is deployed and in active use. Police and fire departments are already using AI for operational tasks. The foundation exists. What is missing is the connective tissue between systems, the governance layer that ensures AI outputs are reliable, and the capacity to scale what has been started.

AI adoption status

Greeley's AI adoption is further along than most cities in its population range. The city has moved past the pilot stage into operational deployment across multiple departments.

GovAI — enterprise generative AI

Deployed enterprise-wide with approximately 200 active users. The platform runs on a ChatGPT backbone with government-specific data protections and is the city's primary generative AI tool for internal staff. Knowledge base agents have been built and connected to: HR benefits information, project management office onboarding and project scoring, an ongoing economic development project, and real estate management (use case in development). Two hundred active users represents meaningful organizational penetration, not a pilot.

Microsoft Copilot — public chatbot

A Microsoft Copilot chatbot is deployed on the city's public website. The team has expressed dissatisfaction with its performance — described as a lower-cost experiment rather than a strategic investment. Citizen-facing AI tools require higher accuracy standards than internal tools, and the current deployment may not meet that bar.

Department-level AI use

The Greeley Police Department is actively using AI for report writing and narration. The Fire Department has expressed interest in similar capabilities. These operational AI deployments exist outside the GovAI platform and represent additional systems that need to be accounted for in the governance framework.

311 virtual agent

The city is rolling out a Zoom-powered call center for 311 services that includes AI virtual agent capabilities, trained on website content. A key concern is accuracy: if the underlying content is outdated or inconsistent, the virtual agent will surface incorrect information to residents — a high-visibility risk that the governance framework in Workstream 4 is designed to address.

Work order management

Public works work order management was identified during the discovery call as one of the most concrete operational pain points. The city processes several thousand work orders per month through Cityworks. Despite workflow streamlining efforts, the triage and assignment process remains manual — a staff member currently spends approximately one hour per day assigning and prioritizing incoming work orders. The challenge is not just volume; it's the decision-making complexity behind prioritization: balancing severity, location, resource availability, cost, historical patterns, and citizen complaint volume across competing demands. This is the specific problem Workstream 2 is designed to address.

Key gaps identified
No unified data layer

Systems operate independently. There is no centralized data warehouse, integration bus, or API orchestration layer. Cross-department data and metrics are produced manually and ad hoc.

No data engineering capacity

The city does not have data engineers on staff. The IT team lacks a designated owner for API connections — even when systems have APIs available, no one is positioned to build and maintain the connections.

No source-of-truth governance

Multiple systems contain overlapping or potentially conflicting data. There is no formal framework defining which system is authoritative, how conflicts are resolved, or how AI-connected data is kept current.

Citizen-facing AI accuracy risk

Without a validation layer and a defined process for keeping their knowledge bases current, the Copilot chatbot and upcoming 311 virtual agent risk providing outdated or incorrect information to residents at scale.

No cross-department visibility

Leadership lacks a unified view of operational metrics across departments. When cross-department data is needed, it is assembled manually from individual systems — limiting the organization's ability to make data-driven decisions.

Investment story gap

The Innovation department has made real progress, but the tools to quantify and communicate that progress to leadership are not in place. During budget reductions, the ability to articulate ROI in concrete terms is essential.

Proposed solution · Phase 1

Phase 1 is designed to accomplish four things simultaneously: understand the full technology and data landscape, deliver a working AI tool that solves a real operational problem, deploy monitoring and reporting dashboards that provide immediate cross-department value, and produce the governance framework and business case Greeley's leadership needs to make informed decisions about future investment. The engagement is structured as four workstreams that run in parallel and feed into each other.

WORKSTREAM 01  Discovery & assessment
Weeks 1–3

Establishes a comprehensive picture of where Greeley stands today across systems, data, and AI readiness. Goes beyond what was discussed during the discovery call by engaging directly with the teams who use these systems daily.

Scope of work
  • Full systems inventory with data flow mapping across Azure, Oracle ERP, Cityworks, ArcGIS, GovAI, Granicus OneView, Trakit, SharePoint, and additional systems
  • API availability audit — which systems expose APIs, which are locked down, where integration opportunities are being left on the table
  • Stakeholder interviews with 3–4 department leads beyond Innovation (IT, public works, 311) to validate pain points and surface additional use cases
  • Data maturity assessment — quality, structure, ownership, and accessibility across departments
  • AI policy and GovAI deployment review, including which agents have been built and where utilization gaps exist
Deliverables
  • Current State Assessment document covering all findings
  • Prioritized use case list ranked by impact, feasibility, and alignment with existing infrastructure
WORKSTREAM 02  Work order prioritization tool
Weeks 3–6

The flagship working deliverable. Rather than ending Phase 1 with a report, this workstream produces a deployed AI tool that solves one of the most concrete pain points identified during discovery — helping public works teams prioritize where to allocate resources across competing demands.

What gets built
  • AI-powered work order triage and prioritization system. Ingests existing work order data from Cityworks and applies prioritization logic based on severity, location, resource availability, estimated cost, historical patterns, and citizen complaint volume. Outputs a ranked priority queue supervisors can review, adjust, and approve before dispatching crews.
  • Human-in-the-loop design. The system recommends priorities; it does not make autonomous decisions. AI performs analysis and surfaces recommendations; humans review and confirm. This keeps prioritization decisions explainable and accountable.
  • Dual-layer accuracy validation. Every AI output passes through two validation layers: a deterministic layer that runs scripted checks against source data, and a secondary AI validation layer that evaluates outputs for consistency, completeness, and logical coherence.
  • Interactive documentation system. A visual, guided interface allowing public works or IT staff to adjust prioritization weights, add new factors, update data connections, and maintain the system independently — designed for non-technical users.
Dependencies & assumptions
  • Cityworks API or data export access. Programmatic access to work order data required. If unavailable, the tool will work with manual data exports as an interim approach, with API integration scoped for a future phase.
  • Structured work order data. Records must include at minimum: category/type, location, submission date, and current status. Additional fields improve the model but are not required for initial deployment.
  • Environment access by Week 2. Access to a Cityworks test/staging environment or representative data export by end of Week 2 to begin development on schedule.
  • UAT participation. 1–2 public works staff members available for user acceptance testing during Weeks 5–6 (~2–3 hours total).

If the Workstream 1 assessment reveals that data access or quality does not support the planned tool design, Mouliqe will present alternative approaches — including a modified scope or budget reallocation toward a different high-impact deliverable — for Greeley's approval before proceeding.

Expected impact
  • Reduction in staff hours spent on manual work order triage and prioritization
  • Fewer mis-prioritized dispatches resulting in crew rework and wasted field time
  • Earlier identification of high-risk maintenance items, reducing emergency escalations
  • More consistent, data-driven resource allocation across public works projects
  • A live, working example of how AI integrates with Greeley's existing infrastructure

WORKSTREAM 03  AI monitoring & reporting dashboards
Weeks 4–7

Delivers three dashboards that provide immediate operational value across the organization. Built on the data connections and system inventory established during Workstream 1, these address three specific gaps: the lack of cross-department visibility, the risk of citizen-facing AI inaccuracy, and the need to maintain GovAI knowledge base quality as adoption scales.

3A · Cross-department reporting dashboard

A unified operational reporting view that pulls data from the systems cataloged in Workstream 1 and presents key metrics across departments in a single interface. Replaces the hours staff currently spend manually assembling reports from individual systems. Provides leadership with a consistent, up-to-date view of operational performance without requiring someone to pull data from Oracle, Cityworks, Granicus, and other systems separately each time a question is asked. Initial data sources and metrics defined during the Workstream 1 assessment; designed to be extensible as additional system connections are established.

3B · 311/Copilot AI accuracy monitor

A monitoring dashboard that periodically tests the responses generated by the Zoom 311 virtual agent and the Microsoft Copilot website chatbot against the city's source-of-truth data. When the monitor detects a response that is outdated, incorrect, or inconsistent with authoritative city information, it flags the discrepancy for review. Addresses the highest-visibility risk identified during discovery: citizen-facing AI tools surfacing incorrect information to residents. Designed to work with the source-of-truth definitions established in the Workstream 4 governance framework.

3C · GovAI knowledge base health dashboard

Monitors the four existing GovAI knowledge bases (HR benefits, PMO onboarding, economic development, real estate management) for content health: stale information, conflicting entries, coverage gaps, and usage patterns. With 200 active users relying on these agents daily, the quality of the underlying knowledge bases directly affects the value the organization gets from its GovAI investment. Surfaces which knowledge bases need attention, which topics generate the most queries, and where users may be getting incomplete or outdated answers.

Dependencies & assumptions

All three dashboards depend on the data access and system connectivity validated during Workstream 1. Scope and depth will be calibrated based on what the assessment reveals about data availability, API access, and data quality. If a specific data source is not accessible within the engagement timeline, the affected dashboard will be delivered with available data sources and documented for expansion when access is established.

WORKSTREAM 04  Governance framework & executive business case
Weeks 5–8

Produces the two documents that make Phase 1 self-sustaining: a governance framework that ensures what was built continues to operate reliably, and an executive business case that gives leadership the information they need to make decisions about continued investment.

AI governance framework
  • Source of truth definitions. Documentation of which systems and datasets serve as authoritative sources for each type of information, and how conflicts between sources are resolved.
  • Data update cadence and ownership. Defined schedules and responsible parties for keeping AI-connected data current, including escalation paths when data falls out of date.
  • Accuracy monitoring protocols. Ongoing checks that ensure AI outputs remain reliable as underlying data changes over time. Operationalized through the 311/Copilot accuracy monitor and GovAI health dashboard delivered in Workstream 3.
  • Extension of existing AI policy. Builds on Greeley's existing AI policy rather than replacing it — adding operational specifics for data governance, validation standards, and maintenance procedures.
Executive business case
  • Current state summary. Concise overview of Greeley's technology landscape, AI maturity, and key gaps, written for a leadership audience.
  • Phase 1 results with measured impact. Documented outcomes from the work order prioritization tool and dashboards, including time savings, efficiency gains, risk mitigation metrics, and qualitative feedback.
  • Future opportunity roadmap. Prioritized next-step opportunities with projected ROI, resource requirements, and recommended sequencing.
  • Investment narrative. A clear articulation of why continued investment in AI and data infrastructure is justified — framed in terms of operational efficiency, service quality, and long-term cost avoidance.

This document is written for the City Manager's office and CIO, not for the Innovation team. It answers the question raised during discovery: "How do we tell the story of where we are, where we want to be, and what it is going to take to get there?"

What comes next

Phase 1 is designed to stand on its own. At its conclusion, Greeley will have a working AI tool, three operational dashboards, a governance framework, and a clear picture of the technology landscape — alongside the assessment and business case needed to evaluate whether and how to proceed with deeper investment.

Based on the discovery conversation and preliminary research, the following opportunities are expected to emerge from the Phase 1 assessment. These are presented as directional context, not as committed scope. The executive business case delivered in Phase 1 will include specific recommendations, projected ROI, and resource requirements for each.

Unified data infrastructure

Connecting Oracle ERP, Cityworks, ArcGIS, Granicus OneView, Trakit, SharePoint, GovAI, and other city systems into a centralized Azure-based data layer. This is the foundational investment that enables AI to work across departments rather than within individual tool silos. The dashboards delivered in Workstream 3 provide a working proof of concept for this vision — Phase 2 would formalize and scale the data connections into a permanent, maintainable infrastructure.

Internal AI agent optimization

Expanding and optimizing the GovAI knowledge base agents Greeley has already begun building. This includes connecting additional data sources, improving agent accuracy through structured knowledge management, and deploying agents to departments that have not yet adopted them. The GovAI health dashboard delivered in Workstream 3 provides the monitoring foundation for this work.

311 virtual agent accuracy and validation

Expanding the accuracy monitoring system delivered in Workstream 3 into a full validation and continuous improvement pipeline for the Zoom-powered 311 virtual agent. Includes automated testing at scale, trend analysis of accuracy over time, and a structured process for updating the agent's knowledge base as city information changes.

Field worker data capture pipeline

A system that allows field crews in public works and public safety to capture notes, observations, and work documentation through simple inputs (such as photos of handwritten notes) that are automatically extracted, structured, and made available for AI-powered querying and reporting. This addresses the gap between desk-based and field-based staff.

Citizen-facing AI rollout strategy

A phased approach to expanding AI-powered services to residents, including dual-option deployment (AI and human channels available simultaneously), community engagement and education, and measurement frameworks for adoption and satisfaction. This accounts for Greeley's resident demographics and the need to build trust incrementally.

Differentiators

Build within, not on top of

Mouliqe does not introduce new platforms. The engagement is designed to work entirely within Greeley's existing technology investments — Azure, Oracle, GovAI, and the tools already deployed across departments. This eliminates the infrastructure cost, vendor management overhead, and adoption friction that come with bringing in unfamiliar systems. The opportunity is to connect and optimize what's already there, not replace it.

Working results, not just reports

Phase 1 does not end with a PDF. It ends with a deployed AI-powered work order prioritization tool, three operational dashboards, a governance framework, and an interactive documentation system, alongside the assessment and roadmap. During budget pressure, it is easier to justify spending on something that is already working and demonstrating value than on a document describing what could be built.

Accuracy validation architecture

AI outputs are only useful if they can be trusted. Mouliqe builds a dual-layer validation system into every AI deployment: a deterministic layer that runs scripted fact-checks against source data, and a secondary AI validation layer that evaluates outputs for consistency, completeness, and accuracy. The 311/Copilot accuracy monitor extends this principle to citizen-facing tools, providing ongoing automated validation that catches issues before they reach residents. This directly addresses the concern raised during discovery about ensuring AI agents are trained on accurate data with a clear source of truth.

Interactive documentation and self-sufficiency

Every system Mouliqe builds comes with an interactive documentation layer designed for non-technical users. Rather than dense technical manuals, this lets internal teams update agent context, modify knowledge bases, and manage data connections through guided, visual interfaces. When the engagement ends, the city's teams should be able to maintain and evolve what was built without calling the consultant back. Capacity building is the priority — not recurring dependency.

Enablement-first framing

This engagement is designed around a principle Greeley's leadership has already articulated: AI is a tool for making people more capable, not a mechanism for reducing headcount. Every solution proposed in this document is evaluated against that standard. If a recommendation does not make an existing team member's job easier or more effective, it does not belong in the scope.
